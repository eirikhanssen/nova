Increasing social inequality - from a uniform to a fragmented social policy?

Forfatter(e):
  • Lars Petter Gulbrandsen
  • John Eriksen
NOVA Rapport 5/01

The 10th Nordic Social Policy Research Conference convened in Oslo in August 2000. The conference was organised by NOVA. This report brings together the seven plenary lectures of the conference. In these lectures, the authors have either taken up the main theme of the conference in a broad comparative perspective or have studied in detail the similarities and differences between two or more Nordic countries. Hopefully, this report will shed light on the state of the Nordic Welfare States at the millennium. Contents:Guri Ingebrigtsen: Increasing social inequality, towards a fragmented social policy.Jørgen Søndergaard: Is increased inequality inevitable?Johan Fritzell: Welfare at a crossroads.Erling Annaniassen: Nordic housing policy: A Scandinavian model or national solutions?Juhani Lehto: West-European trends in social and health care.Janet C. Gornick: Welfare state research and gender: What have we learned?Mikko Kautto and Jon Kvist: Is Nordic social policy remaining distinct or becoming extinct?Roar Hagen: The Nordic welfare state research model stands stable.

Sammendrag av publikasjonen

Introduction

The Tenth Nordic Social Policy Research Seminar was arranged under the title "Increasing social inequality - from a uniform to a fragmented social policy". The seminar was opened by the Norwegian Minister of Social Affairs, Guri Ingebrigtsen, whose presentation was based in the Equitable Redistribution White Paper prepared by the previous government. Accord-ing to the White Paper Norway has seen a positive development in incomes and welfare for the bulk of the population, at the same time as the gap between the richest and poorest has widened. In her presentation Guri Ingebrigtsen writes that over the past 50 years Norway built up a welfare state that caters for the majority of the population. It now faces the major task of lifting an ever poorer and marginalised minority to a higher level. The system of universal benefits and services is no longer sufficient to resolve the problems of this minority.

But can anything at all be done to avoid increasing inequalities? This question is posed by Jørgen Søndergaard, economist and Director of The Danish National Institute of Social Research in Copenhagen. By collating data from several OECD countries, he shows that inequalities in the distribution of market income have risen over the past 10-20 years. This finding also applies to the distribution of household disposable income, although the increased inequality here is far less marked than in the case of individual market income. Government distribution policy via taxes and transfers is a significant contributor to this outcome. Jørgen Søndergaard points out that important forces behind the increased inequality in market income, for example globalisation of capital markets and of parts of the labour market, may lift inequality to a higher level from which it is diffi-cult to find a way back. Neither is it a simple matter to bring about greater equalisation via social and welfare policy since this tends to be accom-panied by disincentives whose greatest impact is on economic participation rates among those at the lowest end of the income distribution scale.

The Swedish government has appointed a commission to study welfare developments in Sweden in the 1990s. The commission comprises seven researchers, one of whom is Johan Fritzell of the Swedish Institute for Social Research. In his presentation he reports preliminary results from, and reflections based on, the work of the commission. In the 1990s sig-nificant changes occurred which at times threatened the economic basis for the Swedish welfare state. For a short period unemployment rose to levels not witnessed since the 1930s. For three years running the gross domestic product contracted, and the influx of refugees was the heaviest seen in the post-war period. All in all he identifies three groups that have emerged in a worse position: immigrants, single mothers and youth. The 1990s were not a child-friendly decade either. Numerous cuts were made in Swedish welfare schemes, but he does not find a basis for asserting that the Swedish welfare state model has undergone a major change, not least because many welfare schemes resumed previous levels in step with the economic re-covery towards the end of the decade.

Are the similarities between the Scandinavian welfare states so marked that the concept of a Nordic welfare state model can be said to rest on a solid empirical base? In the housing sector at any rate, there are significant differences between the countries. This is the starting point for the pre-sentation by historian Erling Annaniassen from NOVA in Oslo. He primarily contrasts Norway and Sweden, two highly similar neighbouring countries with fairly different housing policy solutions. He discusses the differences both in a long and a short-term perspective. Historically speaking the two countries differ widely both in terms of property size and property distribution, with larger properties and relatively speaking fewer owners in Sweden than in Norway. Moreover, important housing policy decisions made in the two countries in the inter-war period differed since the economic crisis hit Norway harder than Sweden. At the same time he points out that the similarities and differences have differing consequences at different points in time. Shortly after the war there was little practical difference between a flat in the Swedish non-profit housing scheme (allmännytta) and a flat in a Norwegian housing co-operative. However, the differing forms of tenure, which were without significance so long as virtually no-one wished to move out of the dwellings, acquired great significance when owner and seller interests associated with an expanding property market spread within the co-operative housing sector in Norway. Based on his studies of the housing sector, Erling Annaniassen is highly sceptical of abstract, theoretical models akin to the Nordic welfare state model.

Juhani Lehto from STAKES in Helsinki bases his presentation on two concepts that have been at centre-stage in recent analyses of the develop-ment of welfare states, namely retrenchment and resiliency. By collating government spending on health, elderly care and child care in four Nordic and four other western European countries, he finds that neither the retrenchment thesis nor the resiliency thesis is sufficient to describe developments in these countries over the past 20 years. Juhani Lehto calls for a third concept: redesign. But he finds at the same time that much of the development is not solely redesign, but design and renewal through expansion of the welfare state.

In her presentation, Janet C. Gornick, Baruch College, City University of New York, gives a broad overview of how gender has been integrated into welfare state scholarship. In specifying the links between gendered and mainstream welfare state theory, she pays special attention to Gøsta Esping Andersen´s The Worlds of Welfare Capitalism from 1990 and the subsequent feminist critique. One line argues that decommodification ap-plies poorly to women´s social and economic circumstances and needs, another that Esping Andersen and other mainstream scholars fail to recognise and conceptualise women´s care work and their unpaid work more generally. Key empirical findings, especially over the last decade, are summarised under four headings: Labour market gaps remain, Welfare states have measurable consequences, Gender gaps in unpaid work are eroding slowly, and Mainstream welfare state typologies need to be modified, but not replaced. The tension within the literature on the welfare state and gender is discussed along two lines: a) employment and strengthen-ing of women´s labour market participation in order to attain full social citizenship, and b) care; stressing the state´s role in supporting women´s care-giving work, by providing care services, guaranteeing women time for care and remunerating care work. In conclusion Professor Gornick sug-gests a road to resolution: a dual earner/dual career model, requiring a major shift in men´s time allocation, a radical restructuring of paid em-ployment, including an end to full-time work as we know it, and an innovative regulatory role for the state.

The Nordic countries opened the 1990s with a marked increase in unemployment. In many quarters the "Nordic welfare model" was seen to be under pressure. In response the Nordic Council of Ministers launched a research programme to consider and reconsider the so-called uniqueness of Nordic policy solutions. This initiative led to two sizeable books entitled "Nordic Social Policy" and "Nordic Welfare States in the European Context", with contributions from 31 Nordic social scientists. The books were pre-sented at the seminar by Mikko Kauto, a member of the editorial group for both books, and Jon Kvist, a member of the editorial group for the latter book. They presented the results of the first volume under the title "No major divergence in politics, surprisingly little changes in welfare". The second volume was presented under the title "Pressure for changes, but only minor convergence". In this volume they address the question of whether a Nordic welfare model still exists. Based on the analysis of two decades of changes they find it unlikely that the Nordic countries will give up their distinct Nordic social policy model or that other countries will adopt the same type and scope of policies prevalent in the Nordic Countries.

The same books are commented on by Roar Hagen in the seminar´s concluding plenary presentation. His point of departure is the title of the final chapter of the second book: "The Nordic welfare state research model stands stable but on shaky ground", which expresses the insight that fluc-tuation in the environment does not necessarily spill over into the political system. Roar Hagen points out that the books view developments in welfare states in the context of social policy, a delimitation he considers narrow and not without significance for the conclusions that can be drawn about welfare states at a general level. He also points to the absence of any consideration of what kinds of societies have welfare states. He recom-mends an alternative approach whose underlying view is that modern society is characterised by increasing specialisation or differentiation and whose main focus is on analysing the mutual independence and depend-ence of the various functional subsystems.
